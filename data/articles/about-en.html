About LGPN-Ling

INTRODUCTION1

The eight volumes of the Lexicon of Greek Personal Names (LGPN) that have appeared to date record all the persons known in Greece, mainland and islands, Asia Minor and up to the far reaches of the Greek-speaking world from the beginning of the alphabetical period down to late antiquity. A total of 350,000 individuals have been identified up to now, all together bearing 35,000 different names, a small proportion of which only are of non-Greek origin. In both print and online versions, the data provided for every bearer of the name include gender, place, date and source.

In this context, the purpose of our project LGPN-Ling, Etymological and Semantic analysis of ancient Greek personal names: electronic and printed dictionary is twofold:

  1. to provide its essential semantical complement to the LGPN Database, which is currently being re-designed to permit such an extension
    (see http://www.lgpn.ox.ac.uk/online/index.html and new http://clas-lgpn4.classics.ox.ac.uk:8080/exist/apps/lgpn/index.html).
  2. to extract from it a printed dictionary, intended to replace the outdated work of Friedrich Bechtel Die Historischen Personennamen des griechischen bis zur Kaiserzeit (1917, hereafter HPN).

The aim is to elucidate the meaning of Greek names, a task which has never before been undertaken except in sporadic fashion and without argument in the outdated work of W. Pape-G. E. Benseler, Wörterbuch der griechischen Eigennamen (1870; hereafter PB) and, for names known from papyri, in the Trismegistos database (http://www.trismegistos.org/nam/search.php). Justifications of proposed meanings will be given, drawn from the various sources available to us: literature, grammarians, epigraphy and papyri.

FOREWORD

a. Reformed alphabet of Ionic-Attic koine

With a view to facilitate query using the database interface and in accordance with the spelling reform of 403 BC under archon Eucleides, we chose to systematically write — no matter the date of their attestation— as η and ω the inherited vowels /e:/ and /o:/, and as ει and ου the so-called spurious diphthongs /e:/ et /o:/ resulting from contraction and compensatory lengthening, a script that was to become that of koine. Whenever a name is a hapax while being attested in pre-Euclidean alphabet, in which E and O denoted the long as well as the corresponding short vowels, or in dialects such as Boeotian or Thessalian, in which the spelling of long vowels of the mid series deviates from this common norm, the original orthography will appear in the form of the constituent theme (for the time being invisible albeit already edited, but intended to be searchable and accessible by means of a drop-down menu).

b. Hierarchization and selection criteria of genuine Greek and Greek hybrid names

α) Lemma vs variant forms

Following Bechtel's HPN practice, we open only one entry grouping all variant forms of a name, whether compound or simple. This entry, or lemma has been chosen arbitrarily as the earliest attested form with the earliest attested gender. However, whenever variant forms collected in diverse dialect areas are exactly contemporaneous, the lemma chosen is either the form documented in the dialect bearing the remotest resemblance to Attic or the form with the less frequently attested gender, viz. the feminine: rarity is then the selection criterion.

  • As a consequence, there is only one entry for pairs (or even triplets) of names differing from each other by common grammatical gender endings, such as the following:
    1. masc. in -ᾱ/ης or -ος vs fem. in -ᾱ/η, -ᾰ or -ον, e.g. Habragoras/Ἁβραγόρας vs Ηabragora/Ἁβραγόρα, Kallinikos/Καλλίνικος vs Kallinike/Καλλινίκη, Habryllos/Ἅβρυλλος vs Habrylla/Ἅβρυλλᾰ and Onesiphoros/Ὀνησίφορος vs Onesiphoron/Ὀνησίφορον, or even the triplet Anchios/Ἄγχιος vs Anchias/Ἀγχίας vs Anchia/Ἀγχία.
    2. fem. in -εια vs masc. in -ύς e.g. Hēdeia/Ἥδεια vs Hēdus/Ἥδυς or vs masc. in -ης, like Eugeneia/Εὐγένεια vs Eugenes/Εὐγένης;
    3. fem. in -ίς vs. masc. in -ις, such as fem. AnaxisἈναξίς vs masc. Ἄναξις;
    4. fem. participles in -ουσα vs masc. -ων, such as Areskousa/Ἀρέσκουσα vs Areskôn/Ἀρέσκων, and in -ασα vs masc. -ας, like Arkesasa/Ἀρκέσασα vs Arkesas/Ἀρκέσας;
    5. em. in -ώ and -οῦς vs masc. in -ων and -οῦς, e.g. Agathô/Ἀγαθώ and Agathous/Ἀγαθοῦς vs Agathôn/Ἀγάθων (cf. Ἀριστώ, daughter of Ἀρίστων, at Milet, first century, and see L. Dubois, in Onomatologos, 402, 407 and 418);
    6. compounds whose masc. second element is -άναξ vs fem. -άνασσα, e.g. Archianax/Ἀρχιάναξ vs Archianassa/Ἀρχιάνασσα.
  • Conversely, the other forms are given as variants of the lemma. A single dialect area is given for each variant, again that of the earliest attestation. If several variants appear to be exactly contemporaneous, the dialect area containing a majority of these, or else the most peripheral area, will be elected,
  • This principle applies in particular to dialectal variants of the roots or bases and suffixes, notably to all containing an /a:/, recorded as α or η depending on dialect or historical period. Accordingly, there is only one entry for Dēmonikos/Δηµόνικος and Dāmonikos/Δᾱµόνικος.
  • It is the same with other types of phonetic/orthographic variants, such as Thess. Eupolos/Εὔπολος, fourth century, subsequently Eukolos/Εὔκολος, second century, or Boeot. Agathoklidas/Ἀγαθοκλίδας, fifth cent. vs Dor. Agathokleidas/Ἀγαθοκλείδας, Hell., or names whose spelling has been affected by postclassical iotacism vs those retaining their etymological spelling. In all these cases, the entry is the earliest attested of the variants.
  • The same goes for compounds whose second element is -κλέᾱς > -κλᾶς vs sigmatic -κλέης < -κλῆς (>-κλέης Χ -έᾱς, see Buck, Greek Dialects, 1955, § 166.1), such as Hēracleas/Ἡρακλέας vs Hēraclas/Ἡρακλᾶς vs  Hēracléês/Ἡρακλέης vs Hēraclês/Ἡρακλῆς.
  • Finally, the same holds for not necessary dialectal alternative forms, e.g. o/ᾱ, the paradigmatic vowel of one of the elements of compounds such as Athenodōros/Ἀθηνό-δωρος (464x) vs Athenādōros/Ἀθηνά-δωρος (1x: Thessaly, second cent.) or ο/ᾰ: these are the the two possible outcomes of the vocalization of a sonant within a zero degree suffix or base, e.g. Stratonike/Στρατο-νίκη (261x) vs Strotonika/Στροτο-νίκα (2x: Boeotia, third cent.)2.

β) Retained types of Greek hybrid names: mixed label Gr.-...

Only the following four categories of Greek hybrid names will be analysed:

  1. Greek base + alien suffix: Gr.-Lat. Pant-auch-ianos/Παντ-αυχ-ιανός
  2. Greek base + alien base or vice versa: Gr.-Thrac. Bendi-dōros/Βενδί-δωρος
  3. Double interpretation names, resulting either from homophony or from interlanguage assonance: Gr. –Iran. Habro-komas/Ἁβρο-κόμᾱς, cf. Iran. *abra ‘rain, clouds’ and kāma ‘wish, will’ « Wolken-Wunsch » [Hinz]?, see R. Schmitt, Die Iranischen und Iranier-Namen in den Schriften Xenophons 2002, p. 41, understood in Greek as ‘whose hair is splendid’).
  4. Hellenized native names subsequently transmitted as Greek names: the Greek anthroponym Abydo-komēs/Ἀβυδο-κόμηςy stems from Ἄβυδος in Troas, a hellenized Anatolian city.

Has not been analysed the reverse of 1) = alien base + Greek suffix: see e.g. for the Thracian area, Dan Dana, in La suffixation des anthroponymes grecs antiques (SAGA 2017), p. 201-223. Semantic calques or interlinguistic synonyms have been left out for future studies.

b. Translation

Only occasionally have names been translated: most of the time we remain at the level of elements of meaning, since the focus is on the name per se - viewed as an abstract entity - rather than on any of its occurrences. In fact, in view of the extension in space and time of the scope of its attestations, a name is likely to have undergone notable variations in terms of meaning or connotation. This is particularly true as the semantic value of their constitutive element(s) could vary from one dialect to another. However, when the name is a paronym of a word of the lexicon or exactly coincides with it, a Logeion link to the LSJ and DGEsp dictionaries gives access to the English and Spanish translations, and sometimes a French translation is added (see 10).

d. Typology of compounds

Given the overrepresentation of Hellenistic and Imperial material, the classification system adopted for compounds has been mainly synchronic (headedness, XYZ labels) rather than resting on inherited syntactic categories (possessives, hypostatics, verbal rection, determinatives)3.

Systematic treatment of anthroponymic onomastics viewed as a lexicon personae is a all the more sensitive matter as this field has been even more creative than for the lexicon at large, especially regarding name compounding and derivation of abbreviated compounds and simplicia. Thus, the logical labelling of structures reshaped by analogical renovation within particular series of compounds has often to remain hypothetical. For instance, regarding compounds whose second element is a verbal adjective in –τος, some were probably coined on the analogy of a reverse structure having a first verbal element in –σιy, such as Lysippos/Λύσ-ιπποςy vs Hippolytos/Ἱππό-λυτος, whence YX<XY, see 8 and n. 5 below . The same holds true for names like archaic Aeol. Abanthis/Ἀβ-ανθίς (<*αυσ-ανθ-, with οὖς ‛ear’ <*aus and ἄνθος ‘flower’), YX(?) vs reverse Cret. Anthôtas/Ἀνθ-ώτας, XY/YX, which are all the more opaque for us as they remain isolated.

2*. Lemma in Greek alphabet, spelt according to Foreword, a. It will later be linked to its bearer(s) in the LGPN database. Concerning the lemma and its variants, see above under Foreword, b, α.
3*. Number of occurrences (automatically extracted from the LGPN database).
4*. Gender: masculine, feminine or both (extracted).
5*. Dialectal/areal and bilingual labels. Bilingual labels deal with the various types of hybrid names that we chose to treat: these hybrids reflect contacts between Greek and another language. Dialectal/areal labels relate to obviously dialectalized names (phonetics/morphology) and names that, albeit belonging to a given dialectal area, lack specific characteristics thereof or are attested when the dialectal period is over. Accordingly, in addition to dialectical labels in the strict sense we use broad geographical labels such as Asia minor, central Greece, Peloponnese, northern Thrace and northern coast of the Black Sea. These allow to substantially reduce the number of other labels. For instance, a name whose earliest attestation is in Arcadia while being also attested elsewhere in the Peloponnese might be simply labelled Pelop., unless it displays typical Arcadian morphology, in which case the label would be: Arcad., Pelop. Up to three labels are employed to characterize each lemma, resulting into the following five labelings:

  1. Label 1 = name attested in one dialect area;
  2. Label 1 + Label 2 = earliest attestation in area 1, highest number of occurrences in area 2;
  3. Label 1 + Label 2 + Label etc. = earliest attestation in area 1, highest number in area 2, and name also attested in area 3;
  4. Label 1 + Label 2 + Label etc. = earliest attestation in area 1, highest number in area 2, and name also attested in at least two more areas;
  5. Label 1 + Label etc. = earliest attestation in area 1, but also in many others.
6*. Chronological range of occurrences of the lemma name (extracted).

Linguistic analysis

7. Segmentation

7.1. Prefixes*: adverbial elements such as negative α(ν)/ν-, copulative (h)α-, αµφ(ι), αν(α), αντ(ι)-, εὐ-6.

7.2. Βases 1* et 2*: Each of these elements, most often borrowed from lexical morphemes and sometimes from non anthroponymic onomastics, is given in a protopypic form – in the most generic possible: e. g. αρχ(ε/ι)- and αρχ(ο/ᾱ)-. In contrast, Bechtel described these analytically in the form of constituents such as « Ἀρχε-, Ἀρχι-, Ἀρχο-, Ἀρχη-, -αρχος zu ἄρχω » (HPN 78). Each of the latter elements, or Constituents, is also edited and will be searchable. Segmentation by hyphens has been guided by word formation whenever possible, with a view to separate the Root in the strict sense from the derivational affixes and suffixes attached to it, viz. those very elements that change it into a Base. The Base in some cases is identical with the Constituent, as in αγαλ-ματ-ι(ο)-, cf. ἀγαλμάτιον ‘small statue’.
At the initial or medial position of the base and at the junction between prefix, base and suffix:

  1. brackets isolate optional phonemes/graphemes (vowels and consonants whose presence or absence varies depending on dialect or chronology). Brackets on both sides of a junction point to a vowel that may belong to the first or the second element depending on whether the perspective is diachronic or synchronic.
    By way of example: Ἁβρῶναξ (<*Ἁβρο-ϝάναξ), a synchronically non-clivable compound (HPN 7): (h)αβρ(ο)- + -(ϝ)ανακ(τ)-.
  2. a slash separates elements apt to alternate according to context (vowels alternating in height, sonants with their diverse vocalizations and consonants variously realized depending on dialect and period of time). Except where alternation is significant (αγορ[ᾱ]- vs ᾱγορ[ο]-, νῑκ[ᾱ/ο] and so forth), we omit to systematically display the ᾱ as a variable alternating with the thematic vowel in the frame of noun inflection. Consequently, a base with its thematic vowel may stand as default base. Last, vowel /a:/ whose sounding evolved into /ε:/ in Ionic-Attic and later koine and came to be written as η is simply noted ᾱ.
By way of example: for Εὐφραῖος, zero-grade φρᾰ- (dat. pl. φρᾰσί, Pind.) of φρήν, φρενός, -φρων, plus suffix *-yo- implies that the name was diachronically segmented -φρα-ιος, whereas synchronically it might be considered as one of the many names in -αῖος. As a result, Εὐφραῖος is edited as ευ + φρ(ον/ᾰ) + (V)ιος.

7.3. Suffixes Up to four suffixes (Φιλ-ων-ιχ-ίδ-ης7) have been arranged from left to right in the tables, that is, in the reverse order of their frequency and importance: the suffix head (Inflect1) of the suffixal chain – a morpheme all the more essential as it is flectional8 - thus ranks 1. The other three are stricto sensu anthroponymic suffixes: they belong to the anthroponym proper rather than to the base of the lexeme underlying the anthroponym, as for instance, -ο-μεν(ο)- in ἀγαλ(λ)-ο-μεν(ο), which is the base of Ἀγαλλομένη since this name was built on participle ἀγαλλόμενος, -η, -ον.
Regarding the Flectional Morpheme (Inflect1), we chose by convention to privilege a synchronic, i.e. the speaker’s, point of view: for him the anthroponymic flectional suffix of a name like Θευδαγόρας is -ᾱς, notwithstanding the fact that this paradigmatic vowel may coincide with that of the derivational suffix constitutive of the base αγορ(ᾱ). For naming his progeny, the speaker may wish to alternate this suffix with e.g. -ίδ-ᾱς or with the corresponding fem. suffix -ᾱ, e.g. in fem. names in - αγόρᾱ. This cell is thus always filled, including for compounds whose second element is of the - ά/ήνωρ type. In these, -ωρ-, albeit etymologically belonging to the base, has been labelled as Inflect1 as well. As expected, Inflect1 is most often the only one represented, either in its simplest form ― e.g. -ς, the nominative of names with second element -(ϝ)ανακ(τ)- ― or more broadly as a desinential complex like -αῖος. The list of the corresponding genitives in La suffixation des anthroponymes grecs antiques (SAGA 2017), p. 691-693, has been updated in the following table.

7.3. Suffixes numbered 4*, 3*, 2* et 1*: stricto sensu. These do not belong to the base of the lexeme underlying the personal name under study (for instance µεν- in ἀγαλ(λ)-ό- µεν-, base of Agallomenē/Ἀγαλλοµεν-ή coined on participle ἀγαλλόµενος, -η, -ον), but they are parts of the very name – in the example above only the flexional suffix -ή). They have been arranged from left to right in the tables, that is, in the reverse order of their frequency and importance: the flexional suffix or head of the suffixal chain thus ranks 1, whereas rank 4 is hardly ever occupied (see Philonichidēs/ΦιλR-ωνS4-ιχS3-ίδS2-ηςS1).

8. Name structure: Functional and syntactic-semantic analysis
8.1. Functional labels: V*(erb) N*(ame) Adj*(ective) Pro*(noun) Adv*(erb), in decreasing order of importance 5 . These labels point to the function of the root(s) or base(s) within the name, such a function itself mirroring the grammatical categories each of them may have developed in the lexicon, in particular through suffixal derivation.
8.1.1. Double labelling of a base within a compound:

V N* = action noun, agent nouns: e.g. ᾱ̆γορ- in Pnyt-agoras/Πνυτ-αγόρας ‘he who pronounces wise words in public’.
V Adj* = verbal adjective 6 , participle: κρι-τ- in Abaiokritos/Ἀβαιό-κριτος ‘chosen by (Apollo) Abaios’.
N Adj* = noun adjective: τελ- in Habrotelēs/Ἁβροτέλης ‘he who splendidly accomplishes’ or ‘splendidly accomplished’.
Adj Adv* = adverbialized adjective: µεγ(α)- in Meg-ainetos/Μεγ-αίνετος ‘highly praised, worthy of high praise’.
V Adv* = adverbialized verb: ἀρχ- in Archi-anassa/Ἀρχι-άνασσα ‘Archqueen’, see archiereus/ἀρχιερεύς ‘archpriest’ vs. Arche-laos/Ἀρχέ-λαος ‘the first (or the chief of) the men-at-arms’, wherein ἀρχ- functions like a V.

8.1.2. Labelling of abbreviated compounds and simplicia. Suffixation, being notably developed in the case of such monothematic personal names, has led to endeavour – in this particular case only, this does not hold for compounds – to label the root's or base's function adding to it a suffixal categorization, to the extent that the latter is clearly identifiable. Thus, we have:
-N = any name whose root is a noun provided with an exclusive noun suffix in the lexicon, in particular if this name is a homonym of a lexical item such as Boeotian Kapos/Κᾶπος (Ion.- Att. κῆπος ‘garden’) or a derivative belonging to another proper name category (theonym, toponym and so forth), e.g. Athēnô/Ἀθηνώ.
-Adj = an exact homonym of a lexical adjective or any anthroponym consisting of an adjectival root provided with an exclusive adjectival suffix in the lexicon (e. g. -ικός, -(V)ιος, fém. -(ϝ)έσσα, comparatives in -(ί)ων, -τερος) and/or that may be considered as adjectival in anthroponyms (-ίδᾱ/ης, hence -ιδεύς, -(ι)άδᾱ/ης, -ινάδᾱ/ης, -ων(ί)δᾱ/ης, -ῖνος, -ίνᾱ/ης, -ίων, - ιανός and so forth).
-Ν Adj = noun base and adjectival suffix or the reverse.
-Ν Adv* = adverbial base and noun suffix.
-V N = action noun in -σ/τις, -ίᾱ/η, -ώ etc. or agent noun in -εύς (and derivatives in -έᾱς, - ήν), -(τ)ᾱ/ης, -ίᾱ/ης, -ᾶς, -τήρ/-τωρ an so forth, whether in the lexicon or not.
-V Adj = verbal adjective in –τος: participle masc. in -ων, -οντος ; -ᾱς, -αντος ; -µενός and adjectivated verb , e.g. Alexiadēs/Αλεξιάδης.
-V N Adj* = verbal adjective with an N -ώ(ι) suffix, e.g. Phantō/Φαντώ, if not an agent noun, see next.

Μυρτ-ώ, on N µύρτος + nominal S = N.
Πείσ-ων, on V πεισ- + nominal S -ων, -ωνος, not -ων, -οντος = V N.
Φαντ-ώ, on V Adj φαντός or V N -φάντης, φάντωρ + nominal S = V N Adj or V N.

8.2. Syntactic-semantic labels: X* (= head) Y* (tail of XY* or YX*) Z* (tail of ZXY* or ZYX*) 7
8.2.1. These labels apply to di- or tri-thematic compounds and to their abbreviated forms (AbbrC*), the latter being reduced in some cases to a single radical. This label does not preclude that the name may have been originally viewed as a simple: it is meant to indicate that the element(s) of meaning borne by the radical were usually combined with others, in order to coin syntagms or propositions endowed with either a generic motivation (rewarding, propitiatory or apotropaic denomination) or a more specific one (name or nickname referring to some event).
Examples: Eu-arch-ippos/ΕὐZ-άρχ X-ιπποςY, trithematic (ZXY) ‘he who has control over his horse’, or possibly di-thematic with a –ιππος suffix 8 . Theu-d-agoras/Θευ-δ-αγόρας, formal or syntactico-semantic trithematic 9 , with a second element reduced to its initial letter: AbbrC stricto sensu. Kalli-k-ôn/Καλλί-κ-ων (patronym Eu-kratēs/Εὐ-κράτης) (YXAbbrC), dithematic abbreviated likewise, vs Kalli-kratēs/Καλλι-κράτης ‘beautifully strong’ or Kallikydēs/Καλλι-κύδης ‘beautifully glorious’ (YX): AbbrC stricto sensu. Agathias/Ἀγαθίας ‘the good’, simple possibly meant as an AbbrC at the time it was given, vs. Agatho-klēs/Ἀγαθο-κλῆς ‘good glory’ (YX) or Pant-agathos/Παντ-άγαθος ‘all-good’ (YX): AbbrC largo sensu.

8.2.2. Complex or evolving structures
8.2.2.1. Inverted structure relative to a pattern possibly inherited occurring even outside of the Greek language: e.g. XY<YX*.
Examples: Kyn-argos/Κύν-αργος (XY), which structurally is the reverse of the older Ved. pattern r̥jí- śvan- (YX) ‘with fastY dogsX’ 10 . Menes-andros/Μενέσ-ανδρος (XY) vs Av. Nǝrǝmanah, gr. Andromenēs/Ἀνδρο-µένης ‘he who has a hero’sY ardourX’ (YX).
8.2.2.2. Semantic reinterpretation in the course of the history of the name: XY>YX* and YX>XY*.
Examples: Sy-agros/ΣύY-αγροςX originally ‘boarY-hunterX’ reinterpreted as ‘wildY boarX’, a syntagm evoking pugnacity (YX>XY), see ad nom. Eri-ōtos/Ἐρί-ωτος ‘prinkingX his earsY’, cf. Ere-damos/ἘρέX-δαµοςY ‘he who excitesX the peopleY’, reinterpreted as ‘with prickedY earsX’, ἐριY- having specialized as an Adv (XY>YX).
8.2.2.3. More than one reading (simultaneously or successively): XY/YX*.
Ex: Kall-aischros/Κάλλ-αισχρος ‘horriblyY beautifulX’ 11 or ‘beautifullyY horribleX’. Arch-agathos/Ἀρχ-άγαθος: the oldest pattern is the reverse compound Agath-archos/Ἀγάθ-αρχος ‘good leader’ (YX). Inverting the elements may have retained the former meaning or else made it evolve towards ‘he who commands good men’ (XY). Further on, the adverbalization of ἀρχ(ι), now endowed with a superlative sense close to ἀρι-, ἀγα- and so forth may have led to reinterpreting this name as ‘overgood’ (YX), see 8.1.1.
8.2.2.4. So-called irrational secondary compounding (O. Masson, OGS 1, p. 88 [1966]) involving seemingly functional and/or semantically incompatible radicals, to the point of blurring the read direction: XY/YX (default label) Irr*. Ex.: Lysainetē/Λυσ-αινέτη : the expected syntactic compounding XY (λυσ- ‘unlash, liberate’ remaining as a verbal head (X) ist incompatible with the second element (V Adj) ‘he about whom it is talked, praiseworthy‘ in principle also occupying the head position, see HPN 26, unless it is conjectured that the name came to be reinterpreted as ‘the liberation about which it is spoken, that has had so much impact‘, the first element now being related to the action noun λύσις: in this guise it might represent a feminine derivative of the father’s nickname or of an ancestor who liberated the city.

9. Meanings of radicals or bases
As mentioned above under Overview, the name is difficult to translate if viewed as an abstract entity. As a consequence, we chose to suggest one to three meanings for each radical, these meanings corresponding on the whole to lemmata entered in the etymological dictionaries DELG and EDG, see 10. In principle, the three meanings have been arranged from the most generic down to the most specific. If the radical appears in the lexicon as verbal form(s) or noun(s) and have these same values in the anthroponym, the three meanings are intended to cover them all. Thus, αρχ ‘(be) first, leader, magistracy’; αγορ ‘(speak in) assembly, speech’ bring together the values of noun ἀγορά and denominative verb ἀγορεύω ; αιθ ‘light up, blaze, (colour of) fire’ gathers two verbal diatheses and two nominal meanings, the former more generic and the latter more specific. The brakets allow to collect two meanings under a single sense.

Structural and semantic analysis of 3 examples commented above:
Abaio-krit-os/Ἀβαιό-κριτ-ος, N Adj-V Adj (YX) ‘Abaios (Apollo) - choice, chosen’: Abai- is the radical of an epiclesis of Apollo, hence N Adj and the radical of the 2nd element is verbal adj. with passive sense, of verb κρίνω, hence V Adj. The latter is the head X of this syntagm, hence a YX structure. This reading sense favours the sense ‘chosen by/choice of (Apollo) Abaios’.
Ab-anthis/Ἀβ-ανθίς N-N (XY/YX) ‘ear-flower, bloom, blooming’: in this case analysis may hesitate beween a recting nominal syntagm (YX ‘ear flower’ or ‘earring’) and a nominal predicate (ear flower or flower-shaped ear XY). The possibiltiy of double reading is summed up by XY/YX.
Archi-anax/Ἀρχι-άναξ, VAdv-N (XY>YX) ‘(be) first, leader, magistracy’-‘(be) lord, protect’: Archwas originally V and the second-element radical was N. Head X is expected to be the verb, hence XY, but Arch- may also have been employed as an Adv modifying N, hence XY>YX and a shift of meanings from ‘leading lords’ to ‘arch-lord’.
